The Layered Spherical Periodic Table: A Three-Dimensional Cosmological Element Classification System with Spectral Frequency as a Unified Periodic Property

Dave Palmer1
1 Dezionz Solutions LLC, Lake Havasu City, Arizona, United States · founder@dezionz.com

Abstract
The standard two-dimensional periodic table, organized by atomic number and electron configuration since Mendeleev (1869), provides no mechanism for representing the environmental conditions under which elements exist, the positions of antimatter counterparts, or the periodic identity of dark matter. We present the Layered Spherical Periodic Table — a three-dimensional model in which elements are distributed across the surface and interior of a sphere at radial depths corresponding to the planetary pressure and temperature regimes in which they naturally occur. A longitude/latitude coordinate grid is overlaid on the sphere, preserving backward compatibility with standard period and group notation. The back hemisphere is assigned to antimatter elements at mirrored coordinates. An equatorial shell outside the primary sphere is designated for dark matter, assigned the symbol De (Delirium), atomic number Z=?, and a new block classification ø-Block. Hydrogen (Z=1) occupies the north pole as elemental origin; the theoretical element Feynmanium (Z=137) occupies the south pole as the hard boundary of atomic physics. Finally, spectral emission frequency is proposed as a formal periodic property for all elements — a property documented for all known elements and designated unknown (?) for Delirium, establishing its frequency as the primary theoretical target for dark matter detection. A fully functional interactive digital prototype has been developed and is described herein.
Keywords: Periodic Table Spherical Model Dark Matter Antimatter Spectral Frequency Planetary Layers Cosmological Chemistry ø-Block Delirium Chemical Education

1.Introduction

The periodic table of elements is among the most consequential organizational frameworks in the history of science. First formalized by Mendeleev in 1869 and refined throughout the twentieth century, the standard model organizes 118 confirmed elements by atomic number and electron configuration into a two-dimensional grid of periods and groups. Its predictive power for chemical bonding, reactivity, and the discovery of unknown elements is well established.

However, the standard model was designed by and for laboratory chemists working at standard temperature and pressure. It is, by construction, context-free: iron at 298K and iron at Earth's core conditions of 360 GPa and 5,000K occupy the same cell and carry the same properties despite exhibiting fundamentally different structural, conductive, and phase behaviors[1]. No environmental axis exists. No position is reserved for antimatter counterparts of known elements, despite experimental confirmation of antihydrogen and theoretical frameworks for antimatter analogs of all known elements[2]. Dark matter — comprising approximately 27% of the mass-energy content of the observable universe[3] — has no representation whatsoever. And the theoretical physics boundary at Z=137, where the 1s electron orbital velocity equals the speed of light and the standard atomic model fails[4], is structurally invisible in the flat model.

Several alternative periodic table geometries have been proposed, including the Janet left-step table[5], the Benfey spiral[6], and various three-dimensional arrangements organized by electron configuration. None address environmental conditions, antimatter, or dark matter as structural elements of the model.

We present the Layered Spherical Periodic Table — a unified three-dimensional classification model that resolves each of these deficiencies while preserving backward compatibility with standard period and group notation through a latitude/longitude coordinate system. We further propose spectral emission frequency as a formal periodic property, and identify the unknown frequency of the dark matter entry as the primary theoretical target for detection instrumentation.

2.The Model

2.1 Radial Layering: Environmental Conditions as the Primary Axis

The central innovation of the Layered Spherical Periodic Table is the use of environmental pressure and temperature as the primary radial placement axis. Elements are assigned to one of five concentric shells corresponding to the natural planetary layers in which they predominate under gravitational differentiation:

Layer Pressure Temperature Representative Elements
Core >360 GPa >5,000 K Fe, Ni, Os, Ir, Pt, Au, U, Th, W, Re
Deep Mantle 24–360 GPa 2,000–5,000 K Hf, Ta, Cr, Cu, Ag, La, Ce, Bi
Upper Mantle 0.3–24 GPa 500–2,000 K Si, Mg, Ca, Al, Ti, Sc, Zr, Ge
Crust 0.001–0.3 GPa 280–500 K O, C, N, Na, K, P, S, Cl, Li, B
Atmosphere <0.001 GPa 180–320 K H, He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, F

This layering reflects observed geochemical behavior. Siderophile elements — those with affinity for iron — migrate to the core during planetary formation[7]. Lithophile elements form silicate minerals in the mantle and crust. Atmophile elements remain gaseous at low pressures and populate the atmospheric shell. The radial structure of the model is therefore not arbitrary but grounded in the observed distribution of elements in rocky planetary bodies.

Key Insight
The standard periodic table tells you what an element is. The Layered Spherical Periodic Table tells you what an element is, where it exists, and under what conditions — simultaneously.

2.2 The Longitude/Latitude Coordinate System

A critical design requirement of the proposed model is backward compatibility with existing chemical notation and education. A spherical model that discards the period/group address system would face insurmountable adoption barriers. The solution applied here is directly analogous to the geographic coordinate system applied to Earth: the planet has always been a sphere, but the latitude/longitude grid allows anyone trained on flat maps to locate any position with precision. The sphere does not become flat; the grid makes it navigable.

In the Layered Spherical Periodic Table:

  • Latitude corresponds to period. Period 1 elements sit at high northern latitudes; heavier elements progress toward the equator and southern latitudes. Hydrogen (Z=1) is placed at the north pole as the elemental origin. Feynmanium (Z=137) is placed at the south pole as the physics boundary (see §2.5).
  • Longitude corresponds to group. Elements sharing a group (vertical family in the standard table) share a meridian on the sphere. The periodic family relationships of the flat table are fully preserved.
  • Radial depth provides the third axis. The layer assignment communicates environmental context without displacing the period/group address. Any element can be located by the same notation used in standard chemistry: period, group, and now layer.

This coordinate system ensures that a student, researcher, or educator familiar with the standard table can navigate the spherical model immediately, while gaining access to a third axis of information that the flat table cannot provide.

2.3 The Antimatter Hemisphere

The standard table provides no structural position for antimatter elements. Antihydrogen was first synthesized at CERN in 1995 and trapped for measurable durations by 2010[2]; antimatter analogs of heavier elements are theoretically well-established. Their absence from the periodic table represents a structural gap rather than a scientific one.

In the Layered Spherical Periodic Table, the back hemisphere (longitudes 180° through 360°) is assigned to antimatter counterparts of all known elements. Each antimatter element occupies a mirrored position at identical period and group coordinates to its matter counterpart, with longitude offset by 180°. Anti-hydrogen mirrors hydrogen at the north pole of the back hemisphere. The matter and antimatter hemispheres are separated by the equatorial Delirium shell (§2.4), consistent with theoretical models of CP-violation and matter-antimatter asymmetry in the early universe[8].

2.4 The Delirium Equatorial Shell: ø-Block and Dark Matter

At a radial distance outside the atmospheric shell, at the equator of the sphere, sits a single entry designated De — Delirium, with atomic number Z=?. Delirium represents the proposed periodic position of dark matter within the classification system.

The placement is governed by three physical observations:

  1. No electromagnetic interaction. All observational evidence indicates dark matter does not emit, absorb, or reflect electromagnetic radiation[3]. Delirium therefore carries no spectral emission in the known EM range — its frequency entry is designated unknown (see §3).
  2. Gravitational cohesion. Dark matter's only confirmed interaction is gravitational. In the spherical model, the Delirium shell is the outermost structural layer — the scaffold that holds all inner shells in gravitational equilibrium. This mirrors the observed behavior of dark matter halos around visible matter structures[9].
  3. Equatorial separation. The equatorial position of Delirium places it at the boundary between the matter and antimatter hemispheres, consistent with the theoretical role of a dark sector mediator in matter-antimatter asymmetry.

Delirium is assigned to a new block designation: ø-Block. The established s-, p-, d-, and f-block designations are defined by the electron orbital type involved in bonding. The ø-Block designation extends this convention to elements with zero electromagnetic interaction — a logically consistent extension that provides a classification home for dark matter and any future entries with similar properties.

This is, to the authors' knowledge, the first assignment of a periodic table position and block designation to dark matter.

2.5 The Physics Boundary Poles

The north and south poles of the sphere carry special significance in the model. Hydrogen (Z=1) at the north pole represents the elemental origin — the simplest atom, the most abundant element in the universe, the starting point of all atomic chemistry. Its polar position reflects its unique status as the seed from which all heavier elements are ultimately synthesized through stellar nucleosynthesis[10].

The south pole is assigned to the theoretical element with atomic number Z=137, referred to informally as Feynmanium following Richard Feynman's identification of this boundary[4]. At Z=137, the Bohr model predicts that the 1s electron would require an orbital velocity equal to the speed of light — a condition at which the standard framework of atomic physics fails. Z=137 is therefore the hard lower boundary of the model: the wall beyond which the rules of the table no longer apply. The poles give the sphere a narrative axis from elemental origin (north) to physical limit (south) that no flat table can communicate.

3.Spectral Frequency as a Unified Periodic Property

Every known element produces a unique spectral signature — discrete frequencies of electromagnetic radiation emitted or absorbed during electron energy transitions. Spectroscopy based on these signatures has been among the most powerful tools in both chemistry and astronomy for over 150 years: it is how we know the composition of stars, nebulae, and distant galaxies without physical contact[11]. Despite this, spectral emission frequency has never been incorporated as a named, tabulated property within any periodic table model.

We propose that spectral emission frequency be formalized as a periodic property within the Layered Spherical Periodic Table, displayed alongside atomic number, atomic mass, electron configuration, and layer assignment for each element. The data for all known elements is comprehensively documented in spectroscopic literature and requires no new measurement.

Element Identity = { Z, symbol, name, period, group, layer, mass, e⁻ config, λ (spectral frequency) }

For known elements: λ = documented emission spectrum (nm / THz) For Delirium (De): λ = ? ← primary dark matter detection target

The inclusion of frequency as a formal property transforms the table's function. An element is no longer identified solely by its proton count and electron arrangement — it is identified by the frequency at which it announces its presence to the universe. This is not a redundant property; it is the property by which most of what we know about the universe's elemental composition was actually determined.

3.1 The Delirium Frequency and Dark Matter Detection

The scientific consensus holds that dark matter has no electromagnetic interaction and therefore no detectable frequency within the known electromagnetic spectrum[3]. Decades of direct detection experiments — including XENON1T, LUX, and PandaX — have returned null results for the leading dark matter candidates: WIMPs, axions, and sterile neutrinos[12].

We propose an alternative framing. The null results of these experiments are not evidence that dark matter has no frequency; they are evidence that dark matter's frequency, if it exists, lies outside the ranges those instruments were designed to detect. The instruments were built to detect known candidate particles at theoretically predicted cross-sections. No instrument has been designed from the premise that dark matter is a periodic element with an unknown but potentially measurable resonant frequency.

The assignment of a frequency property — currently unknown — to Delirium within the periodic table establishes a precise scientific question: at what frequency, if any, does dark matter oscillate, resonate, or interact? Candidate domains for investigation include:

  • Gravitational wave frequency ranges currently mapped by LIGO and future space-based detectors
  • Sub-Planck oscillation frequencies below the current resolution of quantum measurement
  • Frequency ranges associated with gravitational lensing anomalies at galactic scales
  • Hypothetical dark photon frequencies in extensions of the Standard Model[13]

The question mark assigned to Delirium's frequency is not a gap in the table. It is the most consequential open question in modern physics expressed as a single cell in a periodic model — and it defines the detection target with a precision that no prior framework has provided.

4.Relationship to Prior Art

Several alternative periodic table geometries have been proposed and are distinguished from the present model as follows:

  • Janet Left-Step Table (1928)[5]: Reorganizes the flat table by electron shell filling sequence (Madelung order). Remains two-dimensional. Provides no environmental axis, no antimatter positions, and no dark matter entry.
  • Benfey Spiral Table (1964)[6]: Renders elements on a two-dimensional Archimedean spiral emphasizing block continuity. Adds no environmental or cosmological information.
  • Three-dimensional periodic table proposals (various)[14]: Multiple three-dimensional arrangements have been proposed, all organized by electron configuration. None use environmental pressure/temperature as a radial axis. None incorporate antimatter or dark matter.
  • High-pressure phase research (Oganov et al.)[1]: Extensively documents element and compound behavior under extreme pressure conditions. Does not propose a unified classification model incorporating pressure as an organizational axis.

The Layered Spherical Periodic Table is the first known model to simultaneously: (1) use environmental conditions as the primary radial placement axis; (2) assign structural positions to antimatter elements; (3) provide a periodic position and block designation for dark matter; (4) preserve standard period/group coordinates via a geographic grid system; and (5) incorporate spectral emission frequency as a formal periodic property.

5.Working Prototype

A fully functional interactive digital prototype has been developed and demonstrates the practical viability of the model. The prototype renders a three-dimensional sphere in a standard web browser using HTML5 Canvas with the following implemented features:

  • All documented elements distributed across five planetary layers at computed radial depths
  • Antimatter elements rendered in the back hemisphere at mirrored period/group coordinates
  • Delirium (De) rendered as a pulsing equatorial ring at the Delirium shell radius
  • Hydrogen at the north pole; Feynmanium (Z=137) at the south pole
  • Latitude/longitude grid lines corresponding to period and group coordinates
  • Interactive rotation, layer isolation, and element selection with name, atomic number, layer conditions, and descriptive data
  • Painter's algorithm depth sorting for correct 3D occlusion

The prototype was developed concurrently with the theoretical model and serves as proof of concept for both the scientific framework and its application as an educational tool.

6.Potential Applications

  • Science education: The spherical model communicates the distribution of elements in planetary bodies intuitively and visually, making abstract geochemical concepts accessible at the secondary and undergraduate levels.
  • Planetary and geochemical research: Provides a classification framework for element behavior across pressure-temperature regimes relevant to planetary interiors and exoplanet modeling.
  • Dark matter research: Establishes a periodic framework that frames dark matter detection as a frequency identification problem — a reframing with potential to direct instrumentation design.
  • Astrophysics and cosmology: The inclusion of antimatter and dark matter positions makes the model applicable to cosmological discussions that the standard table cannot support.
  • Interactive visualization: The spherical geometry is natively suited to digital, rotatable 3D interfaces for classroom, museum, and research applications.

7.Conclusions

The standard periodic table is an extraordinary scientific achievement that has served chemistry for over 150 years. It is also, by construction, a laboratory instrument — optimized for predicting chemical behavior at standard conditions, with no framework for the 99.9% of the universe that exists under conditions far removed from a terrestrial laboratory.

The Layered Spherical Periodic Table does not replace the standard model. It extends it into three dimensions, adds the environmental context the standard model omits, provides structural positions for antimatter and dark matter, and proposes spectral frequency as the formal periodic property that unifies elemental identity with detectability. The longitude/latitude coordinate system ensures that nothing already known is lost in the transition.

The assignment of an unknown frequency to Delirium — dark matter's periodic entry — reframes one of the most intractable problems in modern physics as a question with a specific, measurable answer waiting to be found. That reframing may be the model's most consequential contribution.
